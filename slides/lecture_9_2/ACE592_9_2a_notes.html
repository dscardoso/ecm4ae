1 What’s new vs. lecture 8.3?

1.1 From fixed to random coefficients

In lecture 8.3 we assumed every individual shares the same \beta, aka, the standard Multinomial Logit (MNL)

What changes in mixed logit:

  • Individual tastes now vary across the population via random coefficients \beta_i
  • This breaks the IIA property in the aggregate: substitution patterns now reflect similarity of tastes
  • The choice probability requires integrating over the distribution of \beta_i: no closed form, so we will need Maximum Simulated Likelihood (part b)

. . .

What stays the same:

  • Transportation scenario, three modes (bus / car / train), city structure
  • Same Julia packages, same revealed-preference data-generation logic
  • Car remains the outside option (V_{ic} = 0)

1.2 Three random parameters, others fixed

We make three coefficients random:

Coefficient Distribution Scope Why
\beta_{i,\text{price}} Log-normal, always < 0 Generic (price enters both modes) Guarantees negative price effect; WTP always finite
\beta_{i,\text{stops,b}} Normal Bus-specific Heterogeneous valuation of bus service
\beta_{i,\text{stops,t}} Normal Train-specific Heterogeneous valuation of train service
All others Fixed — Keeps MSL tractable; focus on key heterogeneity

Price is generic: one \beta_{i,\text{price}} per individual, shared by bus and train. This reflects the interpretation of price as income lost, which is a person-level trait (the marginal utility of income), not a mode-specific preference.

Stops are alternative-specific: bus and train stops represent different technologies with potentially different valuation profiles. We preserve the idea from 8.3 but adding heterogeneity.

2 Model

2.1 Utility specification

U_{ik} = X_{ik}^\prime \beta_i + \nu_{ik}, \quad \nu_{ik} \sim \text{T1EV}

The individual-specific random coefficients are:

\beta_{i,\text{price}} = -e^{\left(b_p + s_p \cdot \eta_{i,p}\right)}, \quad \eta_{i,p} \sim N(0,1)

\beta_{i,\text{stops,b}} = b_{sb} + s_{sb} \cdot \eta_{i,sb}, \quad \eta_{i,sb} \sim N(0,1)

\beta_{i,\text{stops,t}} = b_{st} + s_{st} \cdot \eta_{i,st}, \quad \eta_{i,st} \sim N(0,1)

All three \eta’s are independent across coefficients and across individuals. The log-normal parameterization for price follows Train (2009, Ch. 6).

2.4 True parameter values

Total: 14 parameters. Compare to 8.3b’s restricted model with 11 parameters: here we have the same 8 alternative-specific fixed coefficients plus 6 random-coefficient parameters (location and scale for price, bus stops, and train stops).

3.5 Drawing individual-level parameters \beta_i

This is the key new step. Here, we draw three independent \eta’s per person: one for price, one for bus stops, one for train stops.

N_r = nrow(data_r)

η_p_r  = randn(N_r)   # draws for price coefficient
η_sb_r = randn(N_r)   # draws for bus stops coefficient
η_st_r = randn(N_r)   # draws for train stops coefficient

# Individual-specific coefficients
β_price_r  = -exp.(b_p  .+ s_p  .* η_p_r)    # always negative (log-normal)
β_bstops_r =  b_sb .+ s_sb .* η_sb_r          # bus stops (normal)
β_tstops_r =  b_st .+ s_st .* η_st_r          # train stops (normal)

# Store temporarily in the DataFrame (dropped before export)
data_r.β_price_i  = β_price_r
data_r.β_bstops_i = β_bstops_r
data_r.β_tstops_i = β_tstops_r;

3.6 Mean utility with individual-specific \beta

Another key change from 8.3: price uses a common individual-specific \beta_{i,\text{price}}, but bus and train stops use separate individual-specific coefficients.

data_r.V_ib = zeros(N_r)
data_r.V_it = zeros(N_r)

for i in 1:N_r
    # Bus utility: intercept + price×β_price_i + bus_stops×β_bstops_i + fixed sociodemographics
    data_r.V_ib[i] = (β_b_fixed[1]
        + data_r.price_bus[i]  * data_r.β_price_i[i]
        + data_r.stops_bus[i]  * data_r.β_bstops_i[i]
        + data_r.density[i]    * β_b_fixed[2]
        + data_r.hhsize[i]     * β_b_fixed[3]
        + data_r.homeown[i]    * β_b_fixed[4])

    # Train utility: intercept + price×β_price_i + train_stops×β_tstops_i + fixed sociodemographics
    data_r.V_it[i] = (β_t_fixed[1]
        + data_r.price_train[i] * data_r.β_price_i[i]
        + data_r.stops_train[i] * data_r.β_tstops_i[i]
        + data_r.density[i]     * β_t_fixed[2]
        + data_r.hhsize[i]      * β_t_fixed[3]
        + data_r.homeown[i]     * β_t_fixed[4])
end

Price enters both alternatives with the same individual coefficient \beta_{i,\text{price}} (the “generic” part). Bus and train stops have separate individual coefficients.

3.8 Simulating choices

Revealed preference conditions are identical to 8.3a (what changed is only how V_{ik} was computed).

# Choose train if U_it > U_ib and U_it > U_ic (= 0)
data_r.d_it = (data_r.V_it .+ data_r.ϵ_it .> 0.0) .&
              (data_r.V_it .+ data_r.ϵ_it .> data_r.V_ib .+ data_r.ϵ_ib);

# Choose bus if U_ib > U_it and U_ib > U_ic
data_r.d_ib = (data_r.V_ib .+ data_r.ϵ_ib .> 0.0) .&
              (data_r.V_ib .+ data_r.ϵ_ib .> data_r.V_it .+ data_r.ϵ_it);

3.12 Implied WTP for a bus stop and a train stop

WTP now varies across individuals because of the random coefficients! For each person i we can compute: \text{WTP}_{i,\text{bus stop}} = \frac{\beta_{i,\text{stops,b}}}{|\beta_{i,\text{price}}|}, \qquad \text{WTP}_{i,\text{train stop}} = \frac{\beta_{i,\text{stops,t}}}{|\beta_{i,\text{price}}|} 

WTP_bus_i   = data_r.β_bstops_i ./ abs.(data_r.β_price_i)
WTP_train_i = data_r.β_tstops_i ./ abs.(data_r.β_price_i)

println("WTP bus stop - Mean: $(round(mean(WTP_bus_i)*100,   digits=2)) \$/yr  " *
        "Median: $(round(median(WTP_bus_i)*100,   digits=2)) \$/yr")
println("WTP train stop - Mean: $(round(mean(WTP_train_i)*100, digits=2)) \$/yr  " *

3.13 Distribution of WTP for bus and train stops

Because |\beta_{i,\text{price}}| is log-normally distributed (lower-bounded away from zero), neither WTP ratio blows up.

  • Specifying \beta_{i,\text{price}} as normal would actually lead to undefined mean WTP because of the positive tail (people with \beta_{i,\text{price}} close to zero have huge WTP).
